Project Overview

Case StudyBrandWeb Development

"You are cute" Jeans is a comprehensive UI/UX and web design project focused on creating a modern, visually engaging, and seamless e-commerce experience for a contemporary denim brand.

The goal was to build a digital storefront that reflects the brand's trendy, youthful, and stylish identity while ensuring a user-friendly shopping journey. The design prioritizes clean layouts, striking imagery, and intuitive navigation to capture the essence of the collection.

The Problem

Online fashion brands traditionally structure their homepage around "new collections" — a layout built around the brand's release calendar, not the customer's actual behavior.

But today's shopper doesn't browse that way anymore. They discover products on Instagram, TikTok, or Pinterest before they ever land on the site, arriving with a specific item already in mind, seen on an influencer or in an outfit post. Forcing them to navigate through "Fall Collection 2026" creates friction between what they already want and what the site shows them first.

Hypothesis: if the site surfaces its most desired/best-selling products (Bestsellers) instead of leading with a collection narrative, it shortens the distance between intent (what the customer already saw and wants) and conversion (purchase).

Research & Insight

  • Shopping behavior has shifted from "discovery on-site" to "discovery on social, validation on-site."
  • Customers convert faster on something they already recognize (seen on social, a bestseller, a popular item) than on something brand-new they have to evaluate from scratch.
  • Conclusion: the homepage's priority shouldn't be "what's new", it should be "what's already socially validated and selling.

Design Decision

Instead of the classic Hero → New Collection → Categories structure, I designed:

  • A direct, emotional hero - "Effortless grit and timeless grace, woven in denim" communicates brand identity in seconds, without relying on explaining a collection.
  • A Bestsellers section placed immediately after the hero - with a scroll-driven reveal (the bestsellers section rises and progressively covers the hero as the user scrolls) — a transition that visually guides the user from "aspiration" (the hero image) to "action" (a product they can buy right now).
  • Hover microcopy on products— "Is this your cute jeans?" — reinforces immediate recognition/identification rather than long exploration.
  • A newsletter card integrated visually into the product grid(instead of a popup or standalone section), so the subscription ask doesn't interrupt the discovery-to-purchase flow.

Why this works

  • Reduces the number of clicks between "landing on the site" and "seeing the product you probably already saw online."
  • Aligns the information architecture with the customer's real funnel (social → recognition → purchase), not the brand's internal release calendar.
  • Preserves brand identity (emotional hero) without sacrificing speed-to-conversion.
